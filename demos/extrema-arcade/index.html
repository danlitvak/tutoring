How to play

Read the curve, fast

A function scrolls past. When a marked point freezes between the gate lines, name it before the timer empties.

Is it a local max or min — just a peak or a valley — or the global max or min, the single highest or lowest point anywhere between the left and right edges of the screen? And watch for traps: some marked points are not turning points at all. Those are neither.

What should I notice?

∩

Peaks and valleys

At a local max the curve rises then falls, so the slope goes from positive to negative. At a local min it is the reverse. Feel the slope flip, do not just look at the height.

≈

The “neither” trap

A marked point can be a decoy. The curve is climbing or falling straight through it — it is not a turning point at all, it only looks important because it is highlighted. That is neither a max nor a min.

▲

Local versus global

A local max is any peak. The global max is the single highest point between the left and right edges of the screen right now — a peak, or the curve at the very edge. Scan the whole screen before you commit.

Take it to paper

For a function you know, find every critical point and sort it.

Solve f′(x) = 0. At each solution, check the sign of f′ just before and just after: positive-to-negative is a local max, negative-to-positive is a local min, and no change is neither. Then compare those heights against f at the endpoints to find the global extremes.
